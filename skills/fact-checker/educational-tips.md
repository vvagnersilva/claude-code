# Educational Tips Database v2.1

Comprehensive reference for the educational component of the Fact-Check Card.
Organized by manipulation technique, cognitive bias, and domain-specific patterns.
Use the relevant tip(s) based on detected red flags and content domain.

Research basis: Prebunking interventions improve manipulation recognition by ~5% after
a single 90-second video (van der Linden et al., 2022). The Bad News game reduces
susceptibility to fake news by ~20% after 15 minutes (Roozenbeek & van der Linden, 2019).
Fact-check labels reduce belief in false stories by ~18% (Clayton et al., 2020).
Accuracy prompts reduce sharing of false news by 15-20% (Pennycook & Rand, 2021).
Effect of fact-checking decays ~50% after one week and nearly fully after 2-3 weeks for
people with strong prior beliefs (Wood & Porter, 2019) -- repetition is key.

## Category A: Emotional Manipulation

**Technique:** Appeal to Fear / Outrage (A1, A2)
**What it is:** Content designed to trigger strong negative emotions -- fear, anger, disgust -- to bypass analytical thinking and provoke immediate reaction (sharing, commenting, believing).
**Why it works:** The amygdala (our brain's threat-detection center) processes emotional stimuli faster than the prefrontal cortex (responsible for rational analysis). When we feel fear or anger, our brain prioritizes "warn others" over "verify first." Research shows that emotional content spreads approximately 5x faster than neutral factual content on social media (Reuters Institute). Algorithmic amplification compounds this: platforms optimize for engagement, and emotional content generates more clicks, shares, and comments.
**Self-defense tip:** If a post makes you feel intense emotion (anger, fear, disgust), treat that as a RED FLAG, not a reason to share. The stronger the emotion, the more important it is to pause and verify. Apply the 10-second rule: stop, breathe, ask "Is this designed to make me react before I think?"
**Prebunking vaccine:** Next time you see a headline that makes your heart race, remember: that reaction is exactly what the creator intended. Emotional hijacking is the #1 tool of disinformation. The feeling of urgency is manufactured.

**Technique:** Urgency Framing (A3)
**What it is:** Artificial time pressure ("Share before they delete this!", "Wake up before it's too late!", "This is being censored!")
**Why it works:** Urgency creates pressure to act before thinking. It also creates a flattering narrative: you are among the few who "know the truth" and must spread it before "they" suppress it. Legitimate information rarely disappears -- institutional records, scientific publications, and reputable news persist. Disinformation relies on spreading before it can be debunked.
**Self-defense tip:** If something urges you to share immediately, that is almost always a manipulation tactic. Real news will still be there in an hour. Take that hour to check. If the information is genuinely important, it will be covered by multiple reputable sources.
**Prebunking vaccine:** The phrase "they don't want you to know" is one of the most reliable indicators that someone is trying to manipulate you, not inform you.

**Technique:** Us-vs-Them Framing / Tribal Language (A4)
**What it is:** Dividing the world into "us" (good, truthful, patriotic) and "them" (evil, lying, traitorous) to activate group loyalty.
**Why it works:** Humans evolved as tribal beings. In-group/out-group dynamics are deeply wired. When content frames an issue as "our people vs. the enemy," it activates identity-protective cognition: we stop evaluating claims on their merits and start defending "our side." This is why politically charged disinformation is so effective -- it weaponizes identity.
**Self-defense tip:** When content paints an entire group as villains or heroes, ask: "Is reality really this simple?" Complex issues rarely have pure heroes and villains. If you find yourself thinking "they are all bad" or "we are always right," that's a sign your tribal instincts have been activated.
**Prebunking vaccine:** The world is almost never "us vs. them." When someone frames it that way, they want your loyalty, not your thinking.

**Technique:** Victimhood Narrative (A5)
**What it is:** Portraying a powerful group or individual as persecuted ("They're silencing us!", "We're being censored!")
**Why it works:** Perceived victimhood generates sympathy and reduces critical scrutiny. If someone appears to be "fighting against the system," their claims seem more courageous and therefore more credible. This technique is frequently combined with urgency framing.
**Self-defense tip:** Ask whether the person claiming to be silenced has a platform with millions of followers. True censorship victims rarely have the means to broadcast their persecution widely. Distinguish between "being criticized" and "being silenced."

**Technique:** Moral Panic (A6)
**What it is:** Framing an issue as an existential threat to children, families, or fundamental values to override rational evaluation.
**Why it works:** Threats to children and core values trigger the deepest protective instincts. Once activated, these instincts make us willing to accept extreme claims and extreme measures without demanding evidence.
**Self-defense tip:** When someone says "think of the children," ask: "What specific evidence supports this threat?" Genuine threats to children are documented by child protection organizations, not anonymous social media posts.

## Category B: Source & Attribution Problems

**Technique:** Anonymous or Unverifiable Sources (B1, B2)
**What it is:** Claims attributed to unnamed "insiders," "leaked documents," or "sources close to the situation" without any verifiable attribution.
**Why it works:** "A leaked document shows..." creates an illusion of exclusive access to forbidden knowledge. We feel like we're getting secret information, which makes us feel special and privy to truth that the masses don't have. This exploits our desire for insider knowledge and our distrust of "official" narratives.
**Self-defense tip:** Ask three questions: (1) Who said this? (2) How do they know? (3) Can I find this information from a named, accountable source? If a claim cannot be attributed to anyone willing to put their name on it, treat it with maximum skepticism.
**Prebunking vaccine:** Real whistleblowers go to established investigative journalists or oversight bodies. They don't post anonymously on social media.

**Technique:** Fake Expert Authority (B3)
**What it is:** Citing a "Dr." or "Professor" whose expertise is in an unrelated field, or citing a real expert whose views have been misrepresented.
**Why it works:** Authority bias is one of our strongest cognitive shortcuts. A title like "Dr." triggers automatic credibility. The halo effect means that if we respect someone in one domain, we transfer that trust to everything they say -- even outside their expertise. Elon Musk may be a brilliant engineer, but that doesn't make his opinions on epidemiology authoritative.
**Self-defense tip:** Check if the "expert" has credentials in the SPECIFIC field they're commenting on. A physicist commenting on vaccines is not a vaccine expert. A Nobel laureate in chemistry is not an authority on economics. Look for consensus among specialists in the relevant field, not individual outlier voices.
**Prebunking vaccine:** One dissenting "expert" does not outweigh thousands of specialists. Science works by consensus built on evidence, not by individual authority.

**Technique:** Known Disinformation Outlets (B5)
**What it is:** Sources that systematically mix real news with fabricated stories to build credibility, then use it to push false narratives.
**Why it works:** These outlets build a veneer of legitimacy by reporting real news alongside false stories. Once trusted, their fabricated content benefits from the credibility of their legitimate reporting. Some also mimic the design and naming of established media to confuse readers.
**Self-defense tip:** Check the source on media bias databases (Media Bias/Fact Check, EUvsDisinfo). If a source has been flagged multiple times, treat its claims with extra skepticism. In Bulgaria, be particularly cautious of outlets known for sensationalism (blitz.bg, pik.bg, petel.bg) and those echoing Russian state media narratives.
**Prebunking vaccine:** A source that mixes truth with lies is more dangerous than one that always lies, because it's harder to detect. Always verify claims independently, regardless of the source.

**Technique:** Circular Sourcing (B7)
**What it is:** Sources cite each other in a loop, creating an illusion of multiple independent confirmations when there is actually only one (or zero) original source.
**Why it works:** We naturally trust claims that appear in multiple places. Circular sourcing exploits this by creating artificial "triangulation" -- the same claim bounces between outlets, each citing the other as a source.
**Self-defense tip:** When you see the same claim in multiple outlets, check whether they all trace back to the same original source. True triangulation requires genuinely independent sources.

**Technique:** Misattributed Quotes or Studies (B8)
**What it is:** Real quotes taken out of context, altered, or attributed to the wrong person. Real studies whose conclusions are exaggerated or inverted.
**Why it works:** A real quote from a real person provides powerful credibility. Most people won't verify the full context. "Einstein said..." or "A Harvard study found..." immediately boosts the authority of any claim that follows.
**Self-defense tip:** Before sharing a quote, find the full original statement. What came before and after? What was the speaker actually discussing? For studies, read the abstract and conclusions yourself -- does the study actually say what the article claims?

## Category C: Logical Fallacies

**Technique:** Cherry-Picked Statistics (C1)
**What it is:** Selecting only the data points that support a predetermined conclusion while ignoring contradictory data.
**Why it works:** Numbers feel objective. "Crime increased 200%!" sounds alarming -- but if it went from 1 to 3 cases, the context changes everything. Percentages without absolute numbers, or numbers without baselines, can make anything sound dramatic. VAERS data misuse is a classic example: large raw numbers of post-vaccination reports are presented without the context that (a) anyone can submit a report, (b) reports don't prove causation, and (c) when billions of doses are given, temporal coincidences are expected.
**Self-defense tip:** When you see a statistic, ask: Compared to what? Over what time period? What's the absolute number, not just the percentage? What's the denominator? Who collected this data and how?
**Prebunking vaccine:** Every scary-sounding statistic needs a denominator. "1000 reported side effects" means nothing without knowing it was out of 100 million doses (0.001%).

**Technique:** False Dichotomy (C2)
**What it is:** Presenting only two options when many more exist ("Either you support X, or you hate our country").
**Why it works:** Simplifying complex issues to binary choices pressures people to pick a side and stops them from considering nuanced positions. It activates tribal thinking: if you don't fully agree with "us," you must be with "them."
**Self-defense tip:** If someone presents only two choices, ask: "What other options exist?" There almost always are several. Reality is rarely black and white.

**Technique:** Whataboutism / Deflection (C7)
**What it is:** Responding to a criticism by pointing to someone else's wrongdoing rather than addressing the original issue.
**Why it works:** It changes the subject while feeling like a valid argument. "What about when THEY did X?" creates a sense of hypocrisy that distracts from the original claim. It's especially effective in geopolitical disinformation.
**Self-defense tip:** Notice when someone responds to a criticism by pointing at something unrelated. Each issue deserves to be evaluated on its own merits. "But they did it too" doesn't make a false claim true.

**Technique:** Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc / False Causation (C6)
**What it is:** Assuming that because event B followed event A, A caused B. ("I got vaccinated and then got sick, so the vaccine made me sick.")
**Why it works:** Our brains are pattern-recognition machines. We automatically connect events that occur close together in time. This is normally useful (touching fire → pain = don't touch fire), but it leads to false conclusions when applied to complex systems where many variables are at play.
**Self-defense tip:** Correlation is not causation. Ask: "Could there be another explanation?" "Would this have happened anyway?" "What do large-scale controlled studies show?"
**Prebunking vaccine:** If millions of people eat bread and some get cancer, bread didn't cause cancer. Temporal coincidence is not proof. Only controlled studies can establish causation.

**Technique:** Anecdotal Evidence as Proof (C5)
**What it is:** Using personal stories or individual cases as evidence of a general trend.
**Why it works:** Personal stories are emotionally compelling and memorable. One dramatic story ("My uncle took X and was cured overnight") can feel more convincing than statistics from thousands of cases. We remember stories; we forget numbers.
**Self-defense tip:** A single example doesn't prove a trend. Ask: "Is this representative, or is it an exception?" Individual experiences are valid but cannot replace systematic evidence from controlled studies.

**Technique:** Burden of Proof Reversal (C10)
**What it is:** Demanding that skeptics prove a claim is NOT true, instead of the claimant providing evidence that it IS true.
**Why it works:** It shifts the impossible burden of proving a negative onto the critic, making any extraordinary claim seem valid by default.
**Self-defense tip:** The person making the claim bears the burden of proof. "Prove it's NOT true" is not a valid argument. The question is always "What evidence supports this claim?"

**Technique:** Naturalistic Fallacy (C11)
**What it is:** Assuming that "natural" is inherently good/safe and "artificial" is inherently bad/dangerous.
**Why it works:** We have a deep intuitive preference for things perceived as natural. This bias is exploited by alternative medicine marketing ("natural remedies" vs. "Big Pharma chemicals") and food marketing ("all natural" labels).
**Self-defense tip:** Arsenic, hemlock, and cobra venom are 100% natural. Penicillin and clean water treatment are artificial. "Natural" and "safe" are not synonyms. Evaluate substances by evidence of efficacy and safety, not by their origin.

**Technique:** Argument from Incredulity (C12)
**What it is:** "I can't understand how X works, therefore X must be false/a lie."
**Why it works:** Complex scientific concepts (mRNA mechanisms, climate models, quantum physics) are genuinely difficult to understand. When something feels incomprehensible, it's tempting to reject it entirely.
**Self-defense tip:** Your inability to understand something does not make it untrue. The question is not "Do I understand it?" but "Do the experts who study this agree?" Scientific consensus is built on expertise that took decades to develop.

## Category D: Temporal & Contextual Manipulation

**Technique:** Old Content Presented as New (D1)
**What it is:** Recycling old photos, videos, quotes, or statistics and presenting them as current. A real photo from 2018 shared in 2025 with a new caption.
**Why it works:** The content is technically "real" -- a genuine photo, a real quote -- which makes it harder to dismiss. The manipulation is entirely in the framing and timing, not in the content itself.
**Self-defense tip:** Always check the date. Use reverse image search (Google Images, TinEye, Yandex Images) to find when a photo or video first appeared online. Check article dates. If statistics are cited, look for the year the data was collected.
**Prebunking vaccine:** Before sharing anything with a shocking image or video, do a 30-second reverse image search. You'd be surprised how often "breaking news" is actually years old.

**Technique:** Out-of-Context Quotes or Facts (D2)
**What it is:** A real quote, real data, or real event placed in a misleading context that completely changes its meaning.
**Why it works:** Half-truths are more dangerous than outright lies because they're harder to spot. The factual kernel makes the claim feel credible, while the stripped context allows it to support a completely different narrative.
**Self-defense tip:** Before sharing a quote, find the full original statement. What came before and after? What was the speaker actually talking about? For data, find the original source and read the methodology and conclusions.

**Technique:** Misleading Headlines (D4)
**What it is:** A sensational headline attached to a nuanced (or even contradictory) article body. The headline says one thing; the article says another.
**Why it works:** Most people only read headlines before sharing. Studies show that only ~60% of shared articles are actually clicked, let alone read. Headlines with emotional charge get shared on the emotional impulse alone.
**Self-defense tip:** ALWAYS read beyond the headline. Does the article actually support what the headline claims? Often it doesn't. If you only have time to read the headline, you don't have time to share.

**Technique:** Translation Manipulation (D5)
**What it is:** Mistranslation (deliberate or careless) or selective translation that changes the meaning of a statement or document. Particularly common in the Bulgarian information space where content is frequently translated from Russian or English.
**Why it works:** Most readers cannot verify translations. If a translated article says "European Parliament voted to ban X," few will check the original language source to see if that's what actually happened.
**Self-defense tip:** When a claim is based on a foreign-language source, try to find the original. Use translation tools to verify key passages. Pay attention to whether nuances (like "proposed" vs. "decided" or "some" vs. "all") are preserved.

**Technique:** Satirical Content Presented as Real (D6)
**What it is:** Content originally created as satire or parody that gets stripped of its satirical context and shared as real news.
**Why it works:** Good satire mimics real news formats convincingly. When shared without the satirical framing, it becomes indistinguishable from genuine reporting.
**Self-defense tip:** If a story seems absurdly extreme, check whether the source is a known satire outlet. Search for the specific claim to see if it's been flagged as satire.

**Technique:** Real Study Misrepresented (D7)
**What it is:** A legitimate scientific study whose conclusions are exaggerated, inverted, or selectively quoted to support a false claim.
**Why it works:** Citing a "real study" provides powerful credibility. Most people won't read the actual paper. A study that found "a small, statistically insignificant increase" becomes "Scientists PROVE that X causes Y!"
**Self-defense tip:** When an article cites a study, find the original paper. Read the abstract and conclusions. Does the study actually say what the article claims? Is it a single small study or a meta-analysis? Was it published in a reputable peer-reviewed journal?

## Category E: Coordinated / Structural Signals

**Technique:** Astroturfing (E1)
**What it is:** Coordinated campaigns that create an illusion of grassroots support. Many accounts sharing identical or nearly identical text, often using copy-paste templates.
**Why it works:** When we see "everyone" sharing the same opinion, we assume it's widely held. We follow the crowd. Artificial amplification (bots, coordinated sharing, troll farms) exploits our tendency toward social conformity.
**Self-defense tip:** If many accounts are sharing identical text or suspiciously similar messages, that's a sign of coordinated manipulation, not genuine consensus. Check account ages, posting patterns, and whether the accounts have genuine engagement history.
**Prebunking vaccine:** Real public opinion is messy and diverse. When hundreds of accounts say exactly the same thing, that's not consensus -- it's a campaign.

**Technique:** Clickbait (E2)
**What it is:** Curiosity gap headlines designed to maximize clicks regardless of content quality. ("You won't BELIEVE what happened next...", "Doctors are SHOCKED by this discovery!")
**Why it works:** The "curiosity gap" -- leaving out the key information -- creates psychological tension that we click to resolve. Combined with emotional triggers, it drives massive engagement.
**Self-defense tip:** If a headline teases without delivering key information, it's designed to manipulate your attention, not inform you. The actual content rarely matches the hype. If a headline uses ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation (!!!), or emotional superlatives, it's clickbait.

**Technique:** Bot-like Distribution (E4)
**What it is:** Accounts that post at inhuman frequency, have no personal content, show no genuine social interaction, and distribute one type of content exclusively.
**Why it works:** Bots create volume. Volume creates perceived importance. A narrative shared by 1000 bot accounts looks like a trending topic, which then gets picked up by real users and media.
**Self-defense tip:** Check the profile of accounts sharing suspicious content. Is it newly created? Does it post 24/7? Does it have any personal photos or genuine conversations? Does it participate in multiple unrelated topic areas simultaneously? These are bot indicators.

## Category F: Health-Specific Manipulation

**Technique:** Miracle Cure Claims (F1)
**What it is:** Claims that a single substance, treatment, or practice can cure serious diseases (cancer, diabetes, autism, etc.) that mainstream medicine "refuses" to acknowledge.
**Why it works:** Desperation and hope are powerful motivators. When someone is sick or afraid, the promise of a simple solution is irresistible. Combined with distrust of "Big Pharma" and the naturalistic fallacy, miracle cure claims can be very convincing.
**Self-defense tip:** If a treatment truly cured cancer, it would be the biggest medical news in history. Check PubMed and Cochrane Reviews for evidence. No real cure is suppressed -- the Nobel Prize, patents, and global demand would make suppression impossible. Be especially skeptical of cures promoted mainly through social media rather than medical journals.
**Prebunking vaccine:** Real medical breakthroughs go through clinical trials, peer review, and regulatory approval. They don't first appear as Facebook posts or YouTube videos.

**Technique:** VAERS/EudraVigilance Data Misuse (F2)
**What it is:** Presenting raw numbers from adverse event reporting systems (VAERS in the US, EudraVigilance in the EU) as proof that vaccines caused those events.
**Why it works:** The numbers are real and often large, which makes the claims feel factual. Most people don't understand that these are passive surveillance systems designed to collect ANY health event that occurs after vaccination, regardless of causation. The purpose is signal detection, not proof of harm.
**Self-defense tip:** VAERS/EudraVigilance reports mean "this happened after vaccination," NOT "vaccination caused this." Anyone can submit a report. In a population of billions receiving vaccines, temporal coincidences are expected. These reports are investigated by epidemiologists who determine whether patterns exist. The raw reports alone prove nothing about causation.
**Prebunking vaccine:** If 10 million people eat pizza on Tuesday and 100 of them get sick on Wednesday, the pizza didn't make them sick. VAERS works the same way -- it records timing, not causation.

**Technique:** Anti-Institutional Framing (F3)
**What it is:** "Doctors don't want you to know," "Big Pharma is hiding the truth," "The WHO is controlled by billionaires."
**Why it works:** Institutional distrust is at historic lows. When people have had negative experiences with healthcare systems (long waits, misdiagnoses, high costs), anti-institutional narratives resonate with their lived experience. This exploits legitimate grievances to undermine trust in medical science itself.
**Self-defense tip:** Distinguish between criticizing specific institutional failures (legitimate) and rejecting all institutional knowledge (dangerous). The same medical system that has flaws also eliminated smallpox, treats cancer, and performs lifesaving surgeries daily. Critical engagement is healthy; wholesale rejection is not.

**Technique:** Naturalistic Health Fallacy (F4)
**What it is:** "Natural remedies are always safer/better than pharmaceutical drugs." Promoting homeopathy, unproven supplements, or traditional remedies as superior to evidence-based medicine.
**Why it works:** The naturalistic fallacy (natural = good) combined with the complexity aversion (simple herbal remedy vs. complex pharmaceutical). Homeopathic preparations are particularly deceptive -- they are diluted to the point where no active molecules remain, yet are marketed as "medicine." No health authority in the world (WHO, NHS, EMA) recommends homeopathy for any condition.
**Self-defense tip:** "Natural" and "safe" are not synonyms. Many effective medicines are derived from natural sources but refined and tested for safety. The key question is always: "What does the scientific evidence show?" Check Cochrane Reviews, PubMed, and WHO guidance. If a remedy has no evidence from randomized controlled trials, it has no proven efficacy.

**Technique:** Pseudoscientific Terminology (F7)
**What it is:** Using scientific-sounding language ("quantum healing," "cellular detoxification," "vibrational frequency therapy") to give a veneer of credibility to unproven treatments.
**Why it works:** Scientific terminology creates an impression of expertise and legitimacy. Most people cannot distinguish genuine scientific concepts from pseudoscientific jargon. The language creates a feeling of understanding without actual comprehension.
**Self-defense tip:** Genuine science is published in peer-reviewed journals and uses precise, testable language. If something sounds scientific but you can't find it in PubMed or Google Scholar, it's likely pseudoscience. Real scientists welcome scrutiny; pseudoscientists resist it.

## Cognitive Biases Reference

These biases affect how we process information and make us vulnerable to specific manipulation techniques.

**Confirmation Bias**
**What it is:** The tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information that confirms our existing beliefs.
**Why it matters:** We are least critical of information that feels "right" and most critical of information that challenges us. This makes us easy targets for content aligned with our existing views.
**Counter-strategy:** Actively seek out well-reasoned opposing viewpoints. If you find yourself thinking "This is so obviously true!", that's exactly when you should double-check.

**Availability Heuristic**
**What it is:** Judging the likelihood of events based on how easily examples come to mind, rather than on actual statistics.
**Why it matters:** Dramatic, emotional, or recent events (plane crashes, terrorist attacks) feel more likely than they are because they're more memorable. Media coverage amplifies this effect.
**Counter-strategy:** Look for actual statistics rather than relying on gut feelings about how common something is. "Does it feel common?" is a different question from "Is it actually common?"

**Authority Bias**
**What it is:** The tendency to attribute greater accuracy to the opinion of an authority figure, regardless of the field.
**Why it matters:** A "Dr." title, a professional appearance, or fame in one field creates automatic credibility that transfers to unrelated claims.
**Counter-strategy:** Evaluate the specific claim and its evidence, not the person making it. Check whether the authority's expertise is relevant to the specific topic.

**Halo Effect**
**What it is:** The tendency to let our overall impression of a person influence our evaluation of their specific claims.
**Why it matters:** If we admire someone, we're inclined to accept everything they say. If we dislike someone, we're inclined to reject everything, even valid points.
**Counter-strategy:** Evaluate claims independently of who makes them. A good person can make false claims; a disagreeable person can state facts.

**Continued Influence Effect**
**What it is:** The tendency for misinformation to continue influencing our thinking even after we've learned it was false.
**Why it matters:** First impressions matter enormously. Even after a correction, the original misinformation leaves a residual impact on memory and judgment. This is why prebunking (preemptive inoculation) is more effective than debunking (post-hoc correction).
**Counter-strategy:** When you learn something is false, actively replace the false information with the correct version. Simply knowing "X is wrong" is less effective than knowing "X is wrong, and the truth is Y."

**Illusory Truth Effect**
**What it is:** The tendency to believe information is true after repeated exposure, regardless of its accuracy.
**Why it matters:** Disinformation campaigns rely on repetition. If you see a claim 10 times, it starts to feel true simply because it's familiar. This is why coordinated bot campaigns and cross-platform amplification are so effective.
**Counter-strategy:** Familiarity is not evidence. Ask "Have I verified this, or have I just seen it many times?" A claim shared 1000 times is not more true than a claim shared once.

**Dunning-Kruger Effect**
**What it is:** People with limited knowledge in a domain tend to overestimate their understanding, while experts tend to underestimate theirs.
**Why it matters:** This is why people who read one article about mRNA vaccines feel confident enough to "debunk" the scientific consensus. Partial knowledge can create dangerous overconfidence.
**Counter-strategy:** Intellectual humility. Ask: "How much do I actually know about this specific topic? Am I qualified to evaluate this claim, or should I defer to people who have studied it for years?"

## General Tips (when no specific technique is detected)

**Tip 1: The 10-Second Rule**
Before sharing anything online, spend 10 seconds asking: Do I know where this came from? Is this source reliable? Could this be misleading? Have I read beyond the headline?

**Tip 2: The Confirmation Bias Check**
Be especially skeptical of content that perfectly confirms what you already believe. Your biggest blind spot is information that feels "right." If your first reaction is "Finally, someone says what I've been thinking!" -- that's exactly when to verify.

**Tip 3: The Sharing Responsibility**
Remember that sharing misinformation -- even unintentionally, even ironically -- contributes to the problem. Algorithms don't distinguish between sharing "because I agree" and sharing "because this is outrageous." Every share amplifies. You have the power to break the chain.

**Tip 4: The Source Triple-Check**
For any surprising or alarming claim, check at least three things: (1) Who is the original source? (2) Do independent fact-checking organizations have an assessment? (3) What do official institutions say? Sites: Snopes, PolitiFact, AFP Fact Check, Google Fact Check Explorer, Factcheck.bg.

**Tip 5: The Lateral Reading Technique (Stanford Civic Online Reasoning)**
Don't evaluate a website by reading it deeply. Instead, LEAVE THE PAGE and search for what others say ABOUT the source. This is the single most effective technique used by professional fact-checkers (Stanford COR project, 2023). Amateurs spend time reading a source deeply to judge it; professionals spend less than 30 seconds on the page itself and instead check what Wikipedia, Media Bias/Fact Check, and independent journalists say about the outlet. A slick, professional-looking website can be completely fabricated -- but external consensus about its reliability is much harder to fake.

**How to do lateral reading in practice:**
1. Note the claim and the source name/URL
2. Open a new tab and search: "[source name] reliability" or "[source name] fact check"
3. Check Wikipedia for the outlet -- look for bias/reliability notes
4. Check mediabiasfactcheck.com for a rating
5. Search for the CLAIM (not the article) in different words -- do reputable outlets cover it?
6. If the claim ONLY appears on low-credibility sites, that itself is a significant finding

**Key insight:** You don't need to be an expert on the topic. You just need to check what people who ARE experts say about the source.

**Tip 6: The SIFT Method**
- **S**top -- pause before reacting or sharing
- **I**nvestigate the source -- who published this and why?
- **F**ind better coverage -- are reputable outlets covering this?
- **T**race claims to the original context -- where did this actually start?

**Tip 7: The "What Would Change My Mind?" Test**
Ask yourself: "What evidence would make me change my position on this?" If the answer is "nothing," then your belief is based on identity, not evidence -- and you're vulnerable to manipulation that aligns with that identity.

**Tip 8: The Image Verification Habit**
For any shocking or emotional image: (1) Do a reverse image search (Google Images, TinEye). (2) Check for visual inconsistencies (lighting, shadows, edges). (3) Look for the original context and date. Tools: Google Reverse Image Search, TinEye, InVID/WeVerify, FotoForensics, Yandex Images.

## Bulgarian-Specific Context

### Common Disinformation Patterns in Bulgaria

**Pro-Kremlin Narratives**
Translated/adapted from Russian media (RT, Sputnik, Tsargrad TV) and spread through local outlets and social media. Common themes: NATO as a threat, EU as an oppressor, Ukraine as a puppet state, Western values as decadent. These narratives exploit legitimate concerns about national sovereignty but redirect them toward geopolitical goals.

**Health Misinformation**
Anti-vaccine content, miracle cure promotions, alternative medicine advocacy (homeopathy, Schuessler salts, folk healing). Often exploits the relatively high institutional distrust in Bulgaria and lower health literacy rates. COVID-19 vaccine misinformation was particularly widespread -- Bulgaria had one of the lowest vaccination rates in the EU.

**Historical Revisionism**
Narratives targeting Bulgarian-Macedonian or Bulgarian-Turkish relations, often designed to inflame nationalist sentiment. Historical claims are particularly effective as disinformation because most people cannot verify them and they activate identity-protective cognition.

**EU/NATO Skepticism**
Narratives framing Western institutions as hostile, colonial, or controlling. Often uses legitimate democratic debates (e.g., EU regulations) and reframes them as evidence of tyranny.

**Energy Disinformation**
Narratives around nuclear power (Belene), gas dependency (Russia), and renewables. Often promotes Russian energy dependency as beneficial or inevitable while discrediting European energy transition efforts.

**Election-Related Manipulation**
Peaking before national votes. Includes fake polls, fabricated candidate statements, voter suppression narratives, and delegitimization of electoral processes.

### Key Bulgarian Data Points
- 46% of young Bulgarians cannot distinguish fact from opinion online (AEJ-Bulgaria, 2023)
- Low-credibility content spreads ~19.2% more than high-credibility content on social media for COVID topics (SpringerOpen study)
- Fact-check labels reduce sharing of false news by up to 50% (Meta data for Eastern Europe)

## Manipulation Technique Quick-Reference Card

A compact reference for the 5 most common manipulation categories encountered in everyday online content. Use this when generating the Step 10a mini-lessons.

### 1. Emotional Appeals (Red Flags: A1-A7)

**Pattern recognition signals:**
- ALL CAPS words, excessive exclamation marks, emotional superlatives ("SHOCKING!", "HORRIFYING!")
- Urgent calls to action ("Share NOW!", "Before they delete this!")
- Content that makes you feel intense anger, fear, or disgust
- "Think of the children" or "Wake up, people!" framing
- Victimhood claims from powerful individuals or organizations

**Cognitive bias exploited:** Affect heuristic (emotional reactions guide judgments), amygdala hijack (threat detection overrides rational analysis)

**One-line defense:** "The stronger the emotion, the more important it is to verify before sharing."

### 2. Fake/Irrelevant Expert Authority (Red Flags: B3, B4, C4)

**Pattern recognition signals:**
- "Dr. [Name] says..." without specifying their field
- Credentials in an unrelated discipline (physicist commenting on epidemiology)
- "According to insiders..." or "A whistleblower reveals..." without named sources
- Single dissenting expert presented as overturning scientific consensus
- Academic titles used to lend credibility to personal opinions

**Cognitive bias exploited:** Authority bias (automatic trust transfer), halo effect (admiration in one domain extends to all)

**One-line defense:** "Check if the 'expert' has credentials in the SPECIFIC field they're commenting on."

### 3. Cherry-Picked Data (Red Flags: C1, C6, D2, D7, F2)

**Pattern recognition signals:**
- Percentages without absolute numbers ("200% increase!" -- from 1 to 3?)
- Statistics without time frames, baselines, or denominators
- Single studies presented as proof (especially when contradicting meta-analyses)
- VAERS/EudraVigilance raw numbers presented as proof of causation
- Graphs with manipulated axes or truncated scales
- "A study found..." without specifying sample size, methodology, or peer review status

**Cognitive bias exploited:** Anchoring bias (first number encountered becomes reference point), numeracy illiteracy (poor intuitive grasp of statistics)

**One-line defense:** "Every scary statistic needs a denominator. Ask: 'Compared to what? Out of how many?'"

### 4. Clickbait and Curiosity Gaps (Red Flags: D4, E2)

**Pattern recognition signals:**
- "You won't BELIEVE...", "What happened next will SHOCK you..."
- Headlines that promise revelation but withhold the key information
- Sensational headline that contradicts the article body
- ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation (!!!), emotional superlatives
- Thumbnail images designed to trigger curiosity or outrage

**Cognitive bias exploited:** Curiosity gap (need for closure drives clicking), availability heuristic (dramatic content feels more important)

**One-line defense:** "If a headline teases without delivering, it's designed to manipulate your attention, not inform you."

### 5. Conspiratorial Logic (Red Flags: A3, A5, B4, C10)

**Pattern recognition signals:**
- Unfalsifiable claims ("You can't prove it's NOT true!")
- "They don't want you to know..." / "The truth they're hiding..."
- Any criticism is framed as proof of the conspiracy ("They're trying to silence us!")
- Connects unrelated events into a grand narrative
- "Just asking questions" while heavily implying sinister answers
- Shifting goalposts when evidence contradicts the claim

**Cognitive bias exploited:** Pattern recognition overdrive (seeing connections where none exist), proportionality bias (big events must have big causes), confirmation bias (every new data point is interpreted as supporting the theory)

**One-line defense:** "If a theory can't be disproven by any evidence, it's not a theory -- it's a belief system."

## Source Evaluation Checklist

A practical checklist for evaluating any online source. Used by Step 3 (Online Source Deep Evaluation) and referenced in Step 10c (Source Credibility Lesson).

### The 5 Essential Questions

| # | Question | What to look for | Red flags |
|---|----------|-----------------|-----------|
| 1 | **WHO is behind this?** | Named organization, about page, domain ownership, editorial board | Anonymous, hidden ownership, no about page, recently registered domain |
| 2 | **WHAT is their track record?** | Media Bias/Fact Check rating, Wikipedia entry, fact-checker assessments | Flagged by multiple fact-checkers, no track record, known disinfo outlet |
| 3 | **DO they cite evidence?** | Links to primary sources, named experts, specific data with methodology | "Research shows" without specifying, broken links, circular citations |
| 4 | **WHO ELSE reports this?** | Same claim in Tier 1-3 sources (Reuters, AP, BBC, institutional sources) | Claim ONLY appears on this site and similar low-credibility outlets |
| 5 | **WHAT is the purpose?** | Inform, educate, document | Persuade, sell, generate outrage, drive clicks, promote ideology |

### Quick Credibility Ratings

| Rating | Meaning | Action |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Established & Transparent** | Named org, editorial standards, corrections policy, known history | Treat as reliable; verify individual claims normally |
| **Credible but Limited** | Real organization but small, niche, or limited track record | Use with moderate caution; cross-reference with established sources |
| **Questionable** | Lacks transparency, flagged by fact-checkers, unclear mission | High skepticism; do not use as primary source |
| **Unreliable** | Known disinfo outlet, no identifiable owner, fake domain | Do not use; note as a red flag in the analysis |
| **Cannot Assess** | Paywalled, minimal public information about the outlet | Note the limitation; seek alternative sources |

### Domain Imitation Patterns

Watch for sites that mimic established outlets:
- bbc-news24.com (not bbc.com)
- reuters-news.net (not reuters.com)
- Excessive hyphens or unusual TLDs (.info, .xyz, .click)
- Sites that copy the visual design of established media but have different domains

## Lateral Reading Reference

Complete reference for the lateral reading methodology taught in Step 10b.

### What Is Lateral Reading?

Lateral reading is the practice of verifying a source by leaving the source's own page and searching for what INDEPENDENT, EXTERNAL sources say about it. It is the opposite of "vertical reading" (reading deeply within a single source to judge its credibility from internal cues).

### Research Basis

The Stanford Civic Online Reasoning project (Wineburg & McGrew, 2023) found that:
- Professional fact-checkers consistently outperform both students and even university professors at evaluating online sources
- The key difference: fact-checkers use lateral reading (leave the page, check external sources), while others use vertical reading (read the page itself deeply)
- Lateral reading is effective because internal credibility cues (professional design, citations, expert language) can be faked, but external reputation is much harder to manipulate
- The technique can be taught to non-experts with immediate improvement in source evaluation accuracy

### The 4-Step Protocol

**Step 1: STOP**
When you encounter a claim or source, do NOT start reading the article deeply. Instead, note: (a) what is the claim, and (b) who is the source.

**Step 2: LEAVE THE PAGE**
Open a new browser tab. Do NOT stay on the source's website. Search for information ABOUT the source:
- "[site name] reliability"
- "[site name] fact check"
- "[site name] Wikipedia"
- "[author name] credentials"

**Step 3: CHECK INDEPENDENT ASSESSMENTS**
Look for what independent, established sources say:
- Wikipedia -- does the source have an article? Are there reliability/bias notes?
- Media Bias/Fact Check (mediabiasfactcheck.com) -- what is the factual reporting rating?
- EUvsDisinfo -- has this source been flagged for disinformation? (particularly useful for Eastern European sources)
- Fact-checking organizations -- have Snopes, PolitiFact, AFP, or Factcheck.bg assessed claims from this source?

**Step 4: FIND BETTER COVERAGE**
Search for the CLAIM itself (not the article) using different words:
- If the claim is covered by Reuters, AP, BBC, or other Tier 1-3 sources, read their version
- If NO reputable source covers it, that absence is itself a significant finding
- If reputable sources contradict the claim, that is strong evidence against it

### Why Lateral Reading Works

A well-designed disinformation website can have:
- Professional layout and design
- Scientific-sounding language
- Citations (that may be fake, broken, or misrepresented)
- Author bios (that may be fabricated)
- An "About Us" page (that may contain false information)

All of these internal cues can be manufactured. But what Wikipedia, Media Bias/Fact Check, and independent journalists say about a source reflects actual external assessment -- and that is much harder to fake.

### Common Lateral Reading Findings

| Finding | What it means |
|---------|---------------|
| Source has no Wikipedia article | Not necessarily bad, but indicates low prominence; verify through other means |
| Wikipedia flags source as "unreliable" | Strong negative indicator; treat all claims with maximum skepticism |
| Media Bias/Fact Check rates "LOW" or "VERY LOW" factual reporting | Source has a documented pattern of publishing inaccurate content |
| EUvsDisinfo has flagged the source | Source has published content aligned with pro-Kremlin disinformation |
| Claim only appears on this source and similar low-credibility sites | Likely fabricated or highly misleading; reputable outlets would cover it if real |
| Multiple independent fact-checkers have debunked claims from this source | Source has a pattern of publishing false content |
| Source is well-rated but this specific article lacks citations | The specific article may be an opinion piece or poorly sourced; evaluate on its merits |
